Easy (and free) Guide to Making Lo-fi Music

  Learning how to make music can be overwhelming since there are too many tools and too much advice online. This guide is intended to simplify the music process and help everyday people get started with free online tools.

  My name is Daniel Wang and I make music as a hobby under my stage name Child Zhu. You can check out my music on all streaming platforms. I will be posting more quick music production hacks and guides so make sure to follow me on instagram at @child_zhu for more music related content.

  We will start by making Lo-fi music since I believe that this genre is one of the easiest types of music to make (as well as easiest to impress your friends). Lo-fi stands for low-fidelity. In music, this means that the low production quality that is usually considered as imperfections is used as a deliberate artistic choice. In recent years, this less energetic genre of music is perfect for people to listen to as background music so they can better concentrate when studying. This is perfect for beginner music producers since the bar is pretty low to make ‘good quality’ lo-fi music. To learn more about the history of lo-fi music, here is a quick summary.

  To start off, we must find a capable digital audio workstation (DAW) so we can easily edit and produce our lo-fi song. Normally, people would buy software to download on their computer such as Logic Pro X, Ableton, or Fruity Loops Studio. However, for an entry-level DAW, it is better to use one that is free and easy-to-use. For this guide, we will be using BandLab which is a free browser DAW. There are many other good free browser DAWs that would also suffice so check them out if you want to.

Start out by creating an account and click the ‘create’ button to start a new project. Your screen should look something like this:

We are going to click the ‘Drum Machine’ button to create a new drum track.

  Before you edit the drum track, make sure to change the bpm (beats per minute) of the song to 70 bpm. The slower tempo is the norm for the lo-fi genre. To change the bpm, click the “120 bpm” display on the top left and type in the tempo that you want (70 for example).

   Now let’s edit the drum track. Since the default drum kit is too bright sounding for lo-fi, we will change it to “Bedroom” by clicking on the Instrument drop down.

   In the grid, deselect everything except the kick and the snare. The default drum loop is too complicated and interesting. Remember, lo-fi music is meant to be in the background and chill. In my example, I put a single kick at the beginning of the measure and a snare hit on the 2 and 4 of the measure.

   Now let’s add a chill piano loop to drive the chords of the song. To keep it easy, we will download a free loop online that is also 70 bpm to match the tempo that we have set. Here is one loop that I found that sounded nice. You can find many free loops of all kinds of instruments by using the Google search bar. If you do find a loop that has a different bpm, make sure to change that in the BandLab project.

   Once you have downloaded the file, add a Voice/Audio track by clicking the “+ Add Track” button on the top left of BandLab. Then clicking on the triple dots on the top right of the Voice/Audio track, click “Import from Disk” and add the file that you just downloaded.

   As you may have noticed, the drum machine has defaulted to give you two 8-bar patterns of ‘A’ and ‘B’. You can edit these patterns by clicking on the corresponding letters on the right of the drum grid. To make the track more musically interesting, I added some hi-hats and more kicks in the B pattern.

You can prolong the piano loop on BandLab by hovering over the top right of the light blue cell and dragging it down to the right.

   Keep playing around with different drum patterns and clicking “Add C” when you are ready to push the pattern onto the project. You can drag and drop the cells to wherever you want. To hear back what you have created, drag the white line (on the measuring stick-like bar) to whichever part of the song and click the play button on the top.

   Now to really make the lo-fi song you made really pop, we will go into mixing and EQ effects to highlight the better qualities of the instruments. To learn more in depth about EQ, here is a good article. For the sake of lo-fi music, we will turn down the high-end frequencies and increase the low-end frequencies so that the sound won’t be too distracting for people who want to listen to it and study.

   Start by selecting the Drum Machine and click the Fx Effects tab on the bottom left. Click “Add Effect” and select the EQ3-M. Turn up the “Low” knob slightly and turn down the “Mid” knob slightly as well. Then turn down the “High” knob significantly. Also, turn down the overall volume of the piano loop to make the kick punch more.

   Congratulations! You have laid down the very basics of a lo-fi song. If you want to go above and beyond, go ahead and play around with adding an instrument track and make some harmonies if you know music theory. The essence of music production is to lay down one track at a time so you don’t overwhelm yourself with trying to do too much at once.
